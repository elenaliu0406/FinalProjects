Introduction

Exploratory Factor Analysis is a technique that is used to reduce data to a smaller set of summary variables and to explore the underlying theoretical structure of the phenomena. It is used to identify the structure of the relationship between the variable and the respondent. This is a strong appeal in analyzing the relationship between behaviors and social science related fields in exploring non-observable traits based on observable variability on behaviors.

Exploratory factor analysis (EFA) is generally used to discover the factor structure of a measure and to examine its internal reliability (latent variables). Exploratory factor analysis has three basic decision points: (1) decide the number of factors, (2) choosing an extraction method, (3) choosing a rotation method.

  1. Decide the number of factors To decide the number of factors, the scree plot is the most common way. Scree plot is a two-dimensional graph with factors on the x-axis and eigenvalue that represents the variance accounted by each latent factors on the y-axis. We can tell from the plot that how much each factors account for total variance (for having the biggest eigenvalues) and decide how many factors supposed to be used in the following steps. The process of decision involves subjective judgement.

  2. Choosing an extraction method There are two mathematical solutions for the extraction method. The first one is PCA, which assumes there is no measurement error existed, which is the true factor analysis. The second one is Maximum Likelihood Estimation. MLE assumes that Factor is with the standard normal distribution. Thus, X (i) N (0, + T(w)* w). This means that the log-likelihood is L = np /2* log (2) n* 2 log | + T(w)* w | n /2 tr(( +T(w) w) ^-1*V ). There are substantial difference between these two approaches, the normally distributed X makes more sense in extracting with the MLE estimation.

  3. choosing a rotation method Rotation is a way that maximizing the higher loading and minimizing the lower ones which to achieve the simplest structure. There are two types of rotations, one is orthogonal rotation, which we used with rotation = varimax in R as an example, but other rotation such as quartamax, equamax are also orthogonal rotation. Orthogonal rotation assumes that there are no correlations between the factors. Oblique rotation, exampled as rotation = oblimin is derived that the factors are somehow correlated, which gives the loading with the correlations between factors as a consideration. Oblique rotation is more practical in the real world for the cases of measurement. For better comparing the higher loadings and lower loadings, we will use varimax rotation method. Varimax rotation removes the middle ground and simplifies the loadings of items.

Advantages of Exploratory Factor Analysis: There are several advantages of EFA. The very first one is its ability on reduction of variables. We can find the inter-relationship of the variables, and dividing them into groups, and combining them into a single, or two factors. Exploratory Factor Analysis can also identify the hidden patterns beyond the observations, which may not be reflected from the direct analysis, and since the interpretation and choice of factors are subjective, its flexibility contributes to the various needs in the social science related analysis.

Data Description

This data set is likert scale data which was collected based on survey questions of factors that influence customers buying decisions while purchasing a car. Customers can give points to the variables and the points are scaled from 1 to 5 with 1 being very low and 5 being very high. There are 90 responses and 14 variables in this data set. The 14 variables are Price, Safety, Exterior looks, Space and comfort, Technology, After sales service, Resale value, Fuel type, Fuel efficiency, Color, Maintenance, Test drive, Product reviews and Testimonials. All the 14 variables are categorical.

The goal for our project is to reduce the variables into numbers of factors so that the dataset will be more manageable. In order to do that, we will conduct factor analysis. There are three packages that will be used during our analysis which are psych, parameters and nFactors. psych is for factor analysis and principal component analysis, parameters is to report results of Barletts sphericity test and nFactors is to determine the number of factors for parallel analysis.

Before conducting factor analysis, we will need to measure the factorability of the data by using Kaiser-Meyer-Olkin(KMO). Once KMOs guideline indicates our dataset is adequate for factor analysis, we will use Barletts sphericity test to see if factor analysis is useful with our data. If the factorbility of the data set is satisfied, we will use three methods, Eigenvalues, Scree plot and parallel analysis, to determine how many factors to be extracted. Next, we will use three functions to conduct factor analysis, which are principal, fa and factanal functions. Different factoring methods will be applied in the function. For example, we will use principal factor analysis and minimum residual. We will also use different rotation methods such as varimax and oblimin, which varimax rotation returns orthogonal factors and oblimin returns non-orthogonal factors.

According to Kaisers guidelines, KMO values between 0.8 and 1 suggest the sampling is adequate, KMO values less than 0.6 indicate the sampling is not adequate for proceeding factor analysis. The KMO we got is 0.61 which is greater than 0.6 so we can conclude that factor analysis is adequate for our sample data.

Barletts Sphericity test

Then, we will apply Barletts sphericity test to check if factor analysis is useful with our data.
Null hypothesis: the variables are orthogonal(not correlated)
Alternative hypothesis: variables are not orthogonal(correlated enough to where the correlation matrix diverges significantly from the identity matrix)
check_sphericity_bartlett(data)
## # Test of Sphericity
## 
## Bartlett's test of sphericity suggests that there is sufficient significant correlation in the data for factor analysis (Chisq(91) = 247.71, p < .001).
Since the p value is smaller than 0.001, we can reject the null hypothesis and conclude that there is sufficient significant correlation in the data for factor analysis. Thus, the factor analysis is useful with our data.

Conclusion

We first checked the factorability of our dataset found that the dataset is adequate for conducting a factor analysis. Then, we used three methods including parallel analysis, scree plot and eigenvalues to determine that we will use 5 factors in our factor analysis. However, for fa(), we tested that 4 factors are adequate with our analysis so we used 4 factors in fa(). To conduct factor analysis, we used three functions, principal, fa and factanal. We conclude that PCA have a better performance among all three analysis because the total variance for PCA method is greater than fa and factanal. We then checked the alpha value of each groups and overall on consistency, and from the output, we acknowledged that the overall consistency for the one with MLE method by fa() function with 4 factors is better than the one with method with 5 factors. The four groups are as following:
- Group1: Price, Resale_Value, Fuel_Efficiency, Maintenance, Product_reviews.
- Group2: Space_comfort, Fuel_Type, Safety, Technology, After_Sales_Service.
- Group3: Color, Exterior_Looks.
- Group4: Testimonials, Test_drive, Product_reviews.
This result makes sense in the real world that Group1 is symbolized with the preference on cost effectiveness, Group 2 is a measurement of reliability, Group 3 is a measure of appearances and Group 4 is a measure of marketing.
